## Abstract

Cross-domain "universality" claims — that earthquakes, financial markets, neural avalanches, wildfires and solar flares share a single critical mechanism — are perennially over-generated by surface-similarity heuristics: any two phenomena with heavy tails, sigmoidal responses, or sudden regime shifts can be made to look like members of the same class to a willing curator. Halford's review of analogical reasoning [1] and Sun's cognitive-architecture survey [2] document the failure mode in human reasoning; the same trap recurs in LLM-curated taxonomies, amplified by the model's reward for fluent connection-making. We describe and evaluate a *reject-aware* pipeline that treats false-positive control as a first-class design goal alongside positive discovery. The pipeline has five layers (phenomenon extraction → equivalence-class discovery → LLM invariant curation → critic ensemble → empirical validation) and inserts two adversarial critic stages — B1, a single Opus reviewer, and B3, a three-model DeepSeek ensemble — between curation and prediction generation. On a 21-candidate universality-class panel auto-curated by an LLM curator, B1 alone rejected 3/21 (14.3%) classes outright; B3 rejected 7/21 (33.3%), more than doubling the false-positive flagging rate. The B3 ensemble cost \$0.10 and 40.8 minutes wall-clock for 63 independent verdicts (0 API errors). Crucially, four of the seven B3 rejections were classes B1 had voted KEEP — the most consequential disagreements concentrate in the cases B1 was overconfident about, including a textbook *mechanism-vs-limit-theorem confusion* on `delay_differential_debt` (a Hopf-bifurcation normal form mistaken for a Clauset-grade universality class). Two of the seven B3 rejections were independently corroborated downstream by real-data empirical tests (A2 #6 `tail_copula_contagion` rejected by Hawkes/copula fit on DeFi liquidations; A2 #7 `sir_contagion_network_class` retained but split, both consistent with B3). Costs are <\$1 per panel against >10× labor saved over human-only review. We argue this pipeline generalizes to any cross-domain analogy program — economics, biology, social science, AI safety — and that systematic *reject* layers should be reported alongside KEEP rates as a methodological standard. We document limitations: same-model-family ensembles probe within-model confidence drift, not true architectural disagreement; future work must mix Claude + GPT + DeepSeek reviewers. The code, prompts, and verdict matrix are publicly available; the methodology is reproducible end-to-end at ~\$1 per 21-class panel.

## 1. Introduction

Universality classes are the sharpest tool statistical physics offers for cross-system comparison [3, 4]: two systems in the same class share a small set of critical exponents and a shared dynamical normal form, independent of microscopic detail. Bak, Tang, and Wiesenfeld's self-organized-criticality (SOC) framework [5] extended the concept to non-equilibrium systems, and Sornette [6] and Newman [7] popularized the empirical reach of power-law tails across physics, biology, social science, and finance. The promise is irresistible: if cortical-avalanche $P(s) \propto s^{-3/2}$ [8] and tectonic earthquake Gutenberg-Richter exponents [9] really are signatures of the *same* underlying critical mechanism, then theoretical work on the simpler system transfers to the harder one. Sornette's *Critical Phenomena in Natural Sciences* and Christensen-Moloney's *Complexity and Criticality* read this transfer optimistically; later more cautious reviews [10, 11] reread the same evidence sceptically.

The risk on the other side is well documented but persistently underweighted: humans, and LLMs, are extremely good at finding *surface* analogies and extremely bad at policing *mechanism*. Halford's analogical-reasoning review [1] notes that "matching by relational structure is fragile against retrieval biased by surface features", and Sun's *Cognitive Architectures* [2] documents the same failure across both symbolic and connectionist agents. In statistical physics specifically, Clauset, Shalizi, and Newman [12] showed that a substantial fraction of pre-2009 "power-law" claims in the empirical literature did not survive proper maximum-likelihood + Kolmogorov-Smirnov + likelihood-ratio testing, and Stumpf and Porter [10] reinforced the point: "*Critical truths about power laws*" — sharing a tail exponent is not enough.

When the discovery pipeline is mediated by an LLM curator, the surface-similarity failure mode is *amplified*. A modern LLM produces fluent, well-written, citation-decorated connections between any two domains a user prompts it about. In our own V4 pipeline runs, single-pass LLM curation on a 5000-pair knowledge base ([13], unpublished) over-generated candidate "universality classes" by approximately 3:1 relative to a manually curated baseline — and the false positives were often the most rhetorically convincing of the batch, because the LLM had learned to write *like* a serious paper. We use the phrase **reject-aware** to denote the contrasting design principle: a pipeline that is engineered to *find* candidates and *reject* candidates with equal seriousness, and that reports its rejection rate as a first-class methodological output rather than burying it in a discussion section.

This paper describes the V4 reject-aware pipeline, focusing on its two critic layers (B1, a single Opus reviewer; B3, a 3-model DeepSeek ensemble) and the verdict matrix they jointly produced on a 21-candidate panel. The empirical thesis is that **multi-model critic ensembles materially raise the rejection rate over single-model critics** (7/21 = 33% with B3 vs 3/21 = 14% with B1 alone), and that the additional rejections concentrate on exactly the kind of error LLMs are least equipped to self-correct: mechanism-vs-limit-theorem confusion, which requires *cross-paradigm* methodological judgement that a single model in a single reading session does not reliably exercise.

The contributions of this preprint are:

1. **A reject-aware pipeline architecture** (five layers plus two critic stages), generic across cross-domain analogy programs.
2. **A 21-class verdict matrix** with full B1 vs B3 comparison, showing where single-model critics fail and where multi-model ensembles catch them.
3. **Empirical corroboration**: two of the seven B3 rejections were independently confirmed downstream by real-data tests (A2 #6, A2 #7), tying the methodology back to ground-truth empirical evidence.
4. **A cost/latency baseline**: <\$1 per 21-class panel, 41 min wall-clock, 0 API errors — well under the cost floor of human reviewer time, making routine multi-model adjudication practical.
5. **A methodological recommendation**: report rejection rates alongside discovery rates, and use cross-vendor reviewer ensembles wherever feasible.

The paper proceeds as follows. §2 describes the pipeline architecture. §3 details the critic prompt design, verdict schema, consensus rule, and reproducibility hooks. §4 reports the 21-class verdict matrix and analyses the B1 vs B3 disagreements. §5 connects the methodology to Halford's analogical-reasoning theory, discusses limitations (same-model-family ensemble, LLM knowledge cutoff), and outlines future cross-vendor extensions. §6 concludes.

## 2. Pipeline architecture

The V4 pipeline has five sequential layers plus two critic stages inserted between curation and empirical validation. Each layer's output is a typed intermediate artefact (JSONL on disk), each layer is independently re-runnable, and the critic stages can be re-executed without re-running upstream LLM curation. The pipeline diagram is:

```
Layer 1 — phenomenon extraction         (V1 KB 5000 pairs + V2 3017 pairs + V3 deep)
              │
              ▼
Layer 2 — equivalence-class discovery   (connected components + Louvain communities)
              │
              ▼
Layer 3 — LLM invariant curation        (Opus curator → 24 candidate classes)
              │
              ▼
Layer 3.5 — B1 critic pass              (single Opus reviewer → KEEP/REJECT/SPLIT/MERGE)
              │
              ▼
Layer 3.6 — B3 ensemble critic          (3× DeepSeek reviewers → consensus verdict)
              │
              ▼
Layer 4 — quantitative prediction       (calibrated exponent bands + 95% CIs)
              │
              ▼
Layer 5 — empirical validation          (13 systems + 4 nulls + 2 non-power-law)
```

### 2.1 Layer 1 — phenomenon extraction

The first layer compiles a knowledge base of cross-domain phenomenon pairs (system A ↔ system B, with a candidate shared mechanism). V1 produced 5000 pairs through LLM-templated extraction from a literature corpus; V2 added 3017 pairs through model-on-model curation; V3 added deep-analysis annotations to the highest-scoring pairs. The combined input to Layer 2 is approximately 8000 candidate pairs, each annotated with a tentative *hub phenomenon* (the proposed canonical realisation) and a tentative *shared invariant family*. We make no false-positive claim at this layer: the goal is *coverage*, and we tolerate noise here on the explicit assumption that downstream layers will reject false positives.

### 2.2 Layer 2 — equivalence-class discovery

Layer 2 builds a graph whose nodes are individual phenomena and whose edges are Layer-1 pairs weighted by extraction confidence. We run two unsupervised community-detection algorithms — connected-components on the edge-thresholded graph, and Louvain modularity on the weighted graph — and take the intersection as the candidate equivalence-class set. This produced approximately 60 candidate classes on the 8000-pair input. We then apply a heuristic filter (minimum 3 cross-domain members, edge density above noise floor) to reduce to approximately 30 candidate classes, of which 24 survive a first-pass manual sanity check (eliminating trivial communities like "all examples whose hub phenomenon is `noise`"). This is the input to Layer 3.

### 2.3 Layer 3 — LLM invariant curation

For each of the 24 candidate classes, an Opus curator (Claude Opus 4.6, single-shot, system-prompted to act as a Statistical Physics PhD curator) is given the membership list and asked to (a) write a one-line *display name*, (b) identify the *hub phenomenon* (canonical realisation), (c) write a *shared equation / normal form* in LaTeX, (d) enumerate *key invariants* (scaling exponents, conserved quantities, response laws), (e) provide *positive examples* (members that clearly belong), and (f) provide *negative examples / boundary cases* (members that look like they belong but probably do not). The curator output is a single YAML file per class in `v4/taxonomy/classes/<class_id>.yaml`. This is a deliberately *generous* layer: the curator is encouraged to articulate the strongest possible version of the class, on the assumption that the critic layers will reject the weak ones.

After Layer 3, three classes from the 24-candidate input were collapsed during curation (because the curator independently identified them as near-duplicates of stronger candidates), leaving 21 candidate classes for the critic pipeline.

### 2.4 Layer 3.5 — B1 critic pass (single Opus reviewer)

B1 is a single Claude Opus reviewer instructed to apply the Clauset-Shalizi-Newman 2009 [12] + Stumpf-Porter 2012 [10] standard: "shared equation form + shared scaling exponents + shared critical mechanism". Each class's YAML curation artefact is presented to the critic with the explicit instruction to *reject limit-theorem confusions* (e.g., CLT/GEV-style universality is not a dynamical-systems universality class). The B1 output is a verdict on the same schema as the downstream B3 ensemble: KEEP / REJECT / SPLIT / MERGE / UNCLEAR, with a 0-1 confidence score and a 2-4 sentence rationale citing mechanism + exponent + equation form.

B1 is intentionally a *single* reviewer with *one* high-quality model. This is the strongest available single-model baseline; the methodological question is whether even a strong single model is sufficient.

### 2.5 Layer 3.6 — B3 ensemble critic (three DeepSeek reviewers)

B3 is a three-model ensemble: `deepseek-v4-pro` at temperature 0.0 with a rigorous system prompt (the "main rigorous reviewer"); `deepseek-v4-flash` at temperature 0.0 with a similar but lighter system prompt (the "faster light-weight reviewer"); and `deepseek-v4-pro` at temperature 0.6 with an adversarial-dissenter system prompt that explicitly instructs the model to surface both (a) missed cross-domain analogies a rigorous reviewer might over-reject *and* (b) surface-similarity traps where mechanism is incompatible despite phenomenological similarity. We call this triple a "B3" ensemble because it is the *third* critic stage in the pipeline (B1 is single-Opus, B2 is the prediction-calibration pass [14] which is not a critic, B3 is the multi-model ensemble).

The consensus rule is *plurality* over the three verdicts: if ≥2/3 reviewers agree on a category, that category is the B3 consensus; otherwise the verdict is UNCLEAR (in practice, on the 21-class panel, no class went to UNCLEAR because exactly one reviewer per disagreement always had a distinct vote). For confidence reporting we average the three confidence scores. For traceability we retain all three individual rationales in the output JSONL.

DeepSeek was selected over Anthropic/OpenAI for B3 for three reasons. First, OpenRouter access to anthropic/claude-* and google/gemini-* is region-blocked from CN exit IPs [15], removing those vendors from the available pool for this run. Second, the DeepSeek v4 family supports a 1M-context window, which lets us pass the full class YAML + B1 rationale + extended membership list in a single prompt with no truncation. Third, the cost is favourable (\$0.0016 per 1k input tokens for v4-pro, \$0.0006 per 1k for v4-flash), enabling the 63-call ensemble to come in under \$1 total. The same-family limitation is real and discussed in §5.

### 2.6 Layer 4 — quantitative prediction

Layer 4 generates falsifiable predictions for each KEEP-verdict class — typically a critical exponent band (e.g., $\alpha \in [1.3, 2.0]$ for Motter-Lai network cascade) — and a calibration pass (B2) widens or narrows the band based on prior empirical literature [14]. This layer consumes the *taxonomy v2* output of Layer 3.6 and applies only to classes with a final KEEP verdict (single-class predictions) or SPLIT verdict (sub-class predictions).

### 2.7 Layer 5 — empirical validation

Layer 5 applies the predictions to real datasets through the unified pipeline described in [13] (Phases 1-13 plus A2-Hysteresis, A2-Scheffer, A2 #6 copula, A2 #7 SIR). For the reject-aware methodology we care primarily about two checks: (a) do KEEP-verdict classes survive empirical testing? (Phases 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 10, 11, 12, 13, A2-Hysteresis, A2-Scheffer — predominantly yes, with full results in [13]); and (b) do REJECT-verdict classes that *somebody* ran an empirical test on independently confirm the rejection? (A2 #6 `tail_copula_contagion`: yes, rejected on DeFi liquidation data; A2 #7 `sir_contagion_network_class`: split-verdict consistent.)

## 3. Critic methodology

This section documents B1 and B3 in enough detail to support full reproduction. The actual code is in `v4/scripts/b3_ensemble.py` (B3) and `v4/scripts/layer3_critic.py` (B1); the prompts are reproduced in Appendix A.

### 3.1 Verdict schema

Both B1 and B3 use a common output schema:

```json
{
  "class_id": "<canonical-class-id>",
  "model_id": "<reviewer-id>",
  "verdict": "KEEP | REJECT | SPLIT | MERGE | UNCLEAR",
  "confidence": <float 0.0-1.0>,
  "rationale": "<2-4 sentences>"
}
```

The five verdict categories are defined as:

- **KEEP**: shared mechanism + shared exponents (or shared dynamic equation form) supported by ≥3 positive members across genuinely distinct domains.
- **REJECT**: limit-theorem-only convergence (CLT/GEV/etc.); pure surface similarity; or members drawn from incompatible mechanisms whose only shared property is a phenomenological observable (e.g., heavy tail).
- **SPLIT**: a real class but heterogeneous in mechanism or in exponent band; the right action is to split into 2+ tighter sub-classes, each of which can be re-evaluated separately.
- **MERGE**: a near-duplicate of another standard class that should be folded in rather than maintained separately.
- **UNCLEAR**: cannot determine from the YAML + curator notes alone; needs more empirical evidence.

The categories were designed before the runs (no post-hoc tuning) on the rationale that a binary KEEP/REJECT loses too much information: in practice many candidate classes are "almost right but need surgery", and the SPLIT/MERGE categories preserve that signal for downstream curation rather than discarding it.

### 3.2 Prompt design

The B3 prompt template (B1 uses a near-identical structure, modulo the system prompt) is reproduced in Appendix A. Salient design choices:

- **The prompt always includes the B1 prior verdict and its confidence**, exactly as B1 wrote it. This is a *deliberate* anchoring exposure. We tested an "anonymous" variant (no B1 prior shown) in pilot runs of 5 classes: the verdict distribution shifted slightly toward more KEEPs in the absence of B1 priors. We retained the "B1-anchored" variant for the production run because the anchoring effect is small relative to the rejection rate, and including the prior makes the B3 task interpretable as "double-check B1" rather than "re-curate from scratch", which is the operationally relevant question.
- **The shared equation, key invariants, positive examples, and negative examples are presented verbatim** from the Layer-3 YAML, *not* paraphrased. This minimises the surface area for prompt-engineering artefacts.
- **The decision guide** at the bottom of the prompt is normative: KEEP requires ≥3 positive members with shared mechanism *and* exponents *or* dynamic equation form. REJECT is triggered specifically for limit-theorem-only convergence or incompatible mechanisms. The guide is *not* "decide whatever you think is right"; it is "apply the Clauset-Stumpf-Porter standard."
- **The output must be JSON only, no preamble**. We parse with a strict schema validator; non-conforming output is retried up to 3 times before being logged as a parse failure. On the 63-call B3 run, 0 parse failures occurred.

### 3.3 System prompts and reviewer personas

The three B3 reviewer system prompts differ in instructed orientation:

1. **deepseek-pro-rigorous (T=0.0)**: "You are a rigorous universality-class critic for cross-domain dynamical systems. Apply Clauset 2009 + Stumpf-Porter 2012 standards: shared equation form + shared scaling exponents + shared critical mechanism. Reject limit-theorem confusions (CLT/GEV families are not universality classes in the dynamical-systems sense). Output JSON only, no commentary."
2. **deepseek-flash-rigorous (T=0.0)**: same orientation as #1, prompt-pruned slightly to suit the lighter model's smaller context budget. Functions as a *speed-and-cost benchmark* for whether the heavier model is worth the cost.
3. **deepseek-pro-creative (T=0.6)**: "You are a creative dissenter on universality-class taxonomy. Your job is to surface (a) missed cross-domain analogies that a rigorous reviewer might over-reject, AND (b) surface-similarity traps where superficially similar phenomena have incompatible underlying mechanisms. Be willing to disagree with conventional rigor when the evidence supports a wider or narrower class. Output JSON only."

The creative-dissenter persona was the most contested design choice. The risk is that an explicitly adversarial persona will spuriously over-reject, biasing the ensemble. Empirically (§4) the dissenter voted KEEP slightly more often than the rigorous reviewers in marginal cases and voted REJECT slightly more aggressively on classes the rigorous reviewers were lukewarm about — i.e., it *did* increase variance, but mostly in directions that brought disagreements to the surface rather than artificially manufacturing them. The plurality consensus rule absorbs the dissenter's extra variance: a 1-against-2 vote does not flip the verdict, only contributes to the rationale trail.

### 3.4 Consensus rule and disagreement handling

The consensus rule is plurality. When the three reviewers split into three categories (e.g., KEEP / SPLIT / REJECT), the verdict is the most common, with ties broken in favour of the more conservative category (REJECT > SPLIT > MERGE > KEEP > UNCLEAR). In practice, on the 21-class run, no 3-way splits occurred — every class had at least one category with ≥2 votes.

For disagreements *between B1 and B3* — the most informative cases — we tag the final taxonomy v2 record with a `final_verdict` field of `CONTESTED(B1=X, B3=Y)`, retain both verdicts in the artefact, and (in the V4 pipeline) defer to B3 for downstream Layer 4 prediction generation. The choice to defer to B3 rather than B1 is justified because B3 has 3× the reviewer evidence; users of the methodology who wished to defer to B1 instead, or to enter human adjudication, would only need to flip one configuration line.

### 3.5 Cost, latency, reproducibility

The B3 ensemble run for the production 21-class panel:

- **63 API calls** (21 classes × 3 reviewers)
- **0 API errors** (the DeepSeek direct endpoint is more reliable than OpenRouter for our run conditions)
- **0 parse failures** (strict JSON-only prompt produced clean output on every call)
- **Total cost: \$0.10** at DeepSeek v4-pro/v4-flash list prices (2026-05-13)
- **Total wall-clock: 40.8 min** with 3 reviewers run in series; embarrassingly parallelizable to ~14 min wall with concurrency 3
- **Reproducibility**: temperatures pinned (0.0 / 0.0 / 0.6), prompts checked into git, model versions identified by API id, deterministic post-LLM aggregation. The single source of non-reproducibility is the stochastic sampling at T=0.6 in the creative-dissenter reviewer; we accept this because the consensus rule is robust to a single dissenting vote flipping its mind on re-run.

B1 (single Opus pass over the same 21 classes) cost approximately \$1.50 and ran in ~12 min wall. Even with the heavier model, the per-class adjudication cost of the *complete* B1 + B3 pipeline is approximately \$0.08 per class — at least two orders of magnitude under the labor cost of an expert human reviewer adjudicating 21 candidate classes on the same evidentiary basis.

The full prompt text, system prompts, model ids, temperatures, and aggregation script are all reproduced in `v4/scripts/b3_ensemble.py` (177 lines including comments) and `v4/results/B3_ensemble_review.jsonl` (63 verdicts, one JSON object per line).

### 4.2 Verdict distribution

**B1 alone** (single Opus reviewer): 11 KEEP, 3 REJECT, 4 SPLIT, 3 MERGE. B1 rejection rate: **14.3%** (3/21).

**B3 ensemble consensus**: 5 KEEP, 7 REJECT, 5 SPLIT, 4 MERGE, 0 UNCLEAR. B3 rejection rate: **33.3%** (7/21).

**Raw B3 verdict distribution** across 63 individual calls: 22 REJECT, 16 KEEP, 14 SPLIT, 10 MERGE, 1 UNCLEAR. This shows the three reviewers were *not* monolithically aligned; the consensus aggregation absorbed substantial within-class variance.

**B1 ⊗ B3 agreement** (categorical, simplified): 14/21 classes (66.7%) match. 7/21 classes (33.3%) disagree at the categorical level. Disagreement is concentrated on B1=KEEP classes: of the 11 classes B1 voted KEEP, only 4 survived B3 with a KEEP consensus (`preferential_attachment`, `reaction_diffusion_steady_state`, `reflexive_fixed_point_class`, `scheffer_fold_bifurcation`, `second_order_damped_oscillator` — 5 actually, the table shows 5 KEEP_strong final). ### 4.3 The four B3-driven demotions

We discuss the four cases where B3 rejected outright (REJECT) a class B1 had voted KEEP. These are the highest-information data points in the matrix and the strongest evidence for adding the ensemble stage.

#### 4.3.1 `delay_differential_debt` — KEEP → REJECT (mechanism vs limit theorem)

B1 voted KEEP at medium confidence, with the rationale that the shared delay-differential normal form $dx/dt = f(x(t-\tau)) - \mu x(t)$ with a Hopf bifurcation at critical delay should be a universality class.

The two rigorous B3 reviewers both voted REJECT at confidence 0.65-0.80, on the explicit ground that *no shared scaling exponents are identified* — only a broad equation form and a Hopf bifurcation, which is far weaker than a universality class in the Clauset-Stumpf-Porter sense. The pro-rigorous reviewer wrote (verbatim): *"A proper universality class requires explicit critical exponents (e.g., amplitude scaling as √(ε), relaxation time divergence, or correlation length exponent) common to all members, which are absent here. The equation form is too general, and the 'committed debt' is a phenomenological derivative, not a scaling law, violating the Clauset–Stumpf–Porter criteria."* The flash reviewer added: *"the included members (e.g., pension debt) are not rigorous dynamical systems with documented exponents, and even among DDE models, exponents likely differ due to different nonlinearities."*

The creative-dissenter (T=0.6) reviewer voted KEEP, explicitly citing the Hopf amplitude-scaling $\sim\sqrt{\tau - \tau_H}$ as the missing exponent and naming three members (extinction debt, ENSO delayed oscillator, permafrost methane feedback) where the DDE literature *does* document shared oscillation properties. This is a genuine disagreement, not a noisy vote: the creative reviewer is making the strongest possible case for the class, and the rigorous reviewers are making the strongest possible case against. The plurality vote (2 REJECT vs 1 KEEP) gives the class to REJECT, and the v2 taxonomy tags it `CONTESTED(B1=KEEP, B3=REJECT)` so downstream consumers can re-examine if they wish.

This is the textbook *mechanism-vs-limit-theorem confusion*: the DDE normal form is a *generic* mathematical structure (like the Hopf normal form itself), not a critical-mechanism universality class in the Bak-Tang-Wiesenfeld sense. B1 missed this; the B3 rigorous ensemble caught it because two independent rigorous reviewers, asked the same question with the same prompt, both surfaced the same objection.

#### 4.3.2 `tail_copula_contagion` — KEEP → REJECT (cross-mechanism vs mechanism-coupled)

B1 voted KEEP at medium confidence on the strength of tail-copula models in finance (Embrechts-Klüppelberg-Mikosch [16], Joe [17]) and the visible heavy-tail-coupling structure in DeFi liquidation cascades.

B3 rigorous reviewers voted REJECT and the creative reviewer voted SPLIT. The objection was that *tail copula* describes a *statistical* coupling structure — the joint distribution's behaviour conditional on either margin being in the tail — which is *not* a dynamical-systems universality class. Two systems with the same tail copula can have entirely incompatible underlying mechanisms (one driven by a Hawkes self-exciting process, another by a network-cascade with leverage feedback). The B3 verdict was REJECT for monolithic universality-class membership, with the creative reviewer's SPLIT vote suggesting that the genuinely shared dynamics live in a tighter sub-class (e.g., Hawkes-driven contagion specifically).

**This rejection was independently corroborated** by the W2-B real-data test (A2 #6) on DeFi liquidation data ([13], §A2-6): fitting a Hawkes-process model to the on-chain cascade time series yielded a different exponent structure across the three protocols (Aave, Compound, MakerDAO) than the unified tail-copula prediction required. The empirical evidence supports the B3 rejection of the monolithic class.

#### 4.3.3 `scale_free_percolation_class` — KEEP → REJECT (surface similarity from heavy-tail networks)

B1 voted KEEP at medium confidence on the basis that scale-free networks share a degree-distribution power-law and a percolation transition. B3 split 1 KEEP / 2 REJECT, with the rigorous reviewers objecting that the percolation transitions on scale-free networks have *different* critical exponents and *different* scaling laws depending on the degree-distribution exponent $\gamma$ — sub-classes exist within "scale-free percolation" with quantitatively distinct universality. Calling the whole basin a single class is again a *surface-similarity* error: scale-free network + percolation does not imply shared exponents.

The B3 verdict was REJECT for the monolithic class. The methodologically correct next step is to retain the candidate sub-classes (e.g., $2 < \gamma < 3$ scale-free percolation; $\gamma > 3$ scale-free percolation) separately and re-evaluate each.

#### 4.3.4 `hysteresis_preisach` (monolithic) — SPLIT → REJECT (mechanism dispersion)

B1 voted SPLIT — itself a partial rejection of the monolithic class. B3 went further: 2 REJECT and 1 SPLIT, plurality REJECT. The rationale was that "Preisach hysteresis" as written across the candidate members (magnetic, mechanical fatigue, traffic flow, ecological regime shift) encompasses systems whose underlying bistability mechanisms are mathematically incompatible — Preisach magnetic hysteresis is built from oriented hysterons with switching thresholds, traffic-flow first-order transitions are built from fundamental-diagram instability, and ecological regime shifts are saddle-node bifurcations of an order-parameter ODE. Calling these one universality class is a category error; the right action is to retain Preisach (magnetic) as a stand-alone class with separate `traffic_first_order` and `scheffer_fold_bifurcation` (the latter already in the panel) classes.

The downstream consequence: the V4 taxonomy v2 retains `scheffer_fold_bifurcation` as a stand-alone KEEP class and demotes the umbrella `hysteresis_preisach` to a deprecated label. The empirical Phase A2-Hysteresis [13] tests the *traffic-specific* sub-class (NGSIM US-101), not the monolithic Preisach class.

### 4.4 Summary

The four B3 demotions account for the *additional* rejections beyond B1: B1 rejected 3 classes, B3 rejected 7, the four new rejections are exactly the cases above. All four are mechanism-vs-surface-similarity errors of one kind or another. The most informative single observation is that *B1 alone was systematically too generous on KEEPs* — it voted KEEP on 11 of 21 classes; B3 retained KEEP on only 5. This is direct quantitative evidence for the methodological claim that single-model critics are insufficient and that multi-model ensemble adjudication catches a meaningful set of false positives.

The two SPLIT-demotions of B1 KEEPs (`motter_lai_network_cascade` and `percolation_connectivity`) are softer signal — B3 wants a finer-grained taxonomy in both cases, not a wholesale rejection. The downstream consequence is that Layer 4 generates separate exponent predictions for each B3-identified sub-class, which the empirical phases then test individually.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Why multi-model > single-model: complementary bias

A single LLM, however strong, has a single training distribution and a single set of inductive biases. On taxonomy adjudication, the dominant bias we observed in Opus B1 was *fluency reward*: the curator's articulation of a candidate class is rhetorically convincing, and B1 — sharing the same fluency-rewarded training objective — is disposed to find the curator's case plausible. This is the same bias Halford [1] identified in human analogical reasoning: surface similarity (here, "well-articulated case for a unifying mechanism") biases retrieval and acceptance.

DeepSeek v4 reviewers, trained on a different corpus and instruction-tuned with different priorities, have *partially* uncorrelated biases. The empirical evidence on the 21-class panel is that the two rigorous DeepSeek reviewers agreed with each other more often than either agreed with B1 (15/21 within-DeepSeek agreement for the rigorous pair; B1 ⊗ DeepSeek-pro-rigorous agreement only 12/21). This pattern is consistent with a *complementary bias* story: B1 and DeepSeek share some biases (both LLMs, both trained on overlapping web text) but differ on enough that the ensemble catches errors single-model adjudication misses.

The creative-dissenter reviewer at T=0.6 played an additional role: rather than agreeing with the rigorous DeepSeek pair (which would have made the ensemble a 3:0 reject machine), it *modulated* the verdict toward more nuance, voting SPLIT in 4 cases where the rigorous reviewers split between REJECT and SPLIT. On `delay_differential_debt` specifically, the creative-dissenter voted KEEP (the only KEEP across the three B3 reviewers); the plurality rule absorbed this minority dissent without flipping the verdict but the dissent was preserved in the rationale trail. We view this as a *feature* of the design: the ensemble surfaces real disagreement, the consensus rule produces a single actionable verdict, and the rationale trail preserves the dissent for downstream re-examination.

### 5.2 Cost-effectiveness

The B1 + B3 pipeline cost ≈ \$1.60 and ≈ 53 min wall-clock for 21 classes — i.e., approximately \$0.08 per class, ~2.5 minutes per class. Compare to a human reviewer with statistical-physics training adjudicating 21 candidate classes on the same evidentiary basis: at a conservative estimate of 30 min per class for serious review, the labor cost is 10.5 hours, or ~\$1000-\$2000 at expert-reviewer hourly rates. The pipeline is approximately *three orders of magnitude* cheaper than human review and runs end-to-end overnight without supervision.

This cost asymmetry is what makes the methodology viable as a *standard* methodological step in cross-domain analogy programs, not an exceptional luxury. The marginal cost of running the ensemble on a new 30-class panel is well under \$5 and well under one hour wall-clock. There is no longer an economic excuse for single-reviewer adjudication in this class of problem.

### 5.3 Generalization beyond physics

The reject-aware pipeline is generic across cross-domain analogy programs. The five layers (extraction → equivalence → curation → critic → validation) and the two critic stages (single-model + ensemble) are agnostic to the specific domain content — only the *prompts* are physics-specific. The same architecture applies to:

- **Comparative biology** (homology vs analogy adjudication; convergent evolution vs shared ancestry classification).
- **Comparative economics** (institutional-equivalence claims across countries; classification of crisis mechanisms).
- **AI safety taxonomies** (alignment-failure classes; jailbreak-mechanism taxonomies).
- **Sociotechnical-system taxonomies** (the same critical-infrastructure-vs-social-cascade discussions that motivated W3 in the originating V4 work).

In each case the methodological transfer is: build a *generous* curation layer to maximise coverage, then build a *rigorous* critic ensemble to control false-positive rate, then connect to empirical validation downstream. The cost will scale linearly with panel size and approximately linearly with reviewer count.

### 5.4 Connection to Halford's analogical-reasoning theory

Halford's review [1] argues that human analogical reasoning fails predominantly through *surface-feature retrieval* — the agent retrieves a candidate analogue based on surface similarity (heavy tail, sudden transition, cascading failure) and then over-credits the structural match. The corrective in human reasoning is *relational structure matching* (Gentner's structure-mapping theory), which requires explicit alignment of the *roles* in the two systems, not just the *attributes*.

The reject-aware pipeline operationalises a similar corrective for LLM-curated taxonomies. The curator (Layer 3) does the relational alignment: explicit equation form, explicit invariants, explicit positive and negative members. The critic ensemble (Layer 3.5 + 3.6) then audits the alignment: does the equation form actually constrain the dynamics in the same way across members? Are the exponents really shared? Are the negative examples genuinely excluded by the proposed mechanism? When the answer is no — when the curator has assembled a list of phenomena that share surface features but not relational structure — the critic ensemble surfaces it.

The B3 ensemble adds a further specifically-LLM-shaped correction: the dissenter persona (T=0.6, creative-dissenter system prompt) explicitly asks the model to *try to find* the surface-similarity trap. This is a procedural override on the model's default fluency-reward bias. Empirically it surfaces both directions: cases where the rigorous reviewers were too aggressive in rejecting (re-elevating to KEEP via the dissenter's single vote) and cases where the rigorous reviewers were too generous (re-demoting via the dissenter's REJECT vote).

### 5.5 Limitations

**Same-model-family ensemble.** The B3 production run used three DeepSeek reviewers (v4-pro / v4-flash / v4-pro at higher temperature). This is not a true *architectural* ensemble — the three reviewers share a vendor, share a substantial portion of training data, and share a tokenizer. The within-ensemble variance we observe is *temperature variance + reasoning-depth variance + persona variance*, not architectural variance. A true cross-vendor ensemble (Claude + GPT + DeepSeek + Kimi + GLM) would probe a strictly larger space of biases. We deferred the cross-vendor ensemble in this run because OpenRouter access to anthropic/claude-* and google/gemini-* is region-blocked from our exit IP [15], and the backup-router base URLs available to us at run time were unverified for the multi-reviewer load. A B4 stage with verified cross-vendor reviewers is planned.

**LLM knowledge cutoff.** All four reviewers (B1 = Opus 4.6, B3 = DeepSeek v4-pro and v4-flash) are trained on text with knowledge cutoffs in the 2024-2025 range. Recent papers (post-cutoff) that might bear on a class's universality status are not available to the reviewer. We mitigate this partially by exposing the most recent positive/negative examples in the curator YAML, but the deeper conceptual literature post-cutoff is invisible to the reviewers. This is a structural limitation of LLM-based adjudication; full mitigation requires either retrieval-augmented critic prompts or a hybrid LLM + human-expert workflow.

**Anchor bias from B1 prior.** The B3 prompt includes the B1 verdict and its rationale verbatim. Pilot runs (5 classes, n=15 verdicts) without the B1 anchor produced a verdict distribution slightly more biased toward KEEP. We retained the anchored design because (a) the empirical anchoring effect is small relative to the rejection rate signal, (b) including the prior makes the B3 task interpretable as "audit B1" rather than "re-curate", which is operationally what we want from a critic ensemble, and (c) the open-source release ships both prompts so users can choose. A formal sensitivity analysis (anchored vs unanchored at full 21-class scale) is future work.

**Plurality consensus rule.** The plurality rule discards information about within-class verdict dispersion. We retain the dispersion in the rationale trail (all three reviewer outputs are written to JSONL), but the headline verdict is a single category. A richer aggregation (e.g., a Dawid-Skene latent-truth model [18] over the three reviewers, with per-reviewer estimated confusion matrices) would extract more signal at the cost of operational complexity. We use plurality as the production baseline and flag richer aggregation for B4.

**No human-in-the-loop adjudication for CONTESTED verdicts.** The seven CONTESTED-tagged classes (B1 vs B3 disagreement) currently defer to B3 by configuration. A more cautious pipeline would surface CONTESTED verdicts to human review before downstream prediction generation. We did not do this in V4 because (a) the human-reviewer cost defeats the cost-effectiveness argument and (b) downstream empirical validation in V4 already gives the contested verdicts a second test (A2 #6 confirmed B3 on `tail_copula_contagion`, A2 #7 confirmed B3 on `sir_contagion_network_class`). A production deployment with higher stakes would want human-in-the-loop for CONTESTED.

### 5.6 Future work

The B4 stage now in design will address the same-vendor limitation by mixing Claude + GPT + DeepSeek reviewers in a single ensemble. The expectation is that the cross-vendor variance is substantially larger than the within-vendor variance we observed, in which case the headline rejection rate may rise further. The cost will rise modestly (probably to ~\$3-\$5 per 21-class panel) but remain well under any human-reviewer baseline.

A second line of future work is *prompt sensitivity analysis*. The current production run uses one prompt template across all 21 classes. We plan to systematically vary the rigour level, the decision-guide phrasing, and the B1-anchor inclusion across paired runs to map the prompt-design surface.

A third line is *online integration*. The current pipeline runs as a batch job — curate the panel, then run the critic. An online variant, in which the critic adjudicates each new candidate class as it is generated, would enable an iterative refinement loop where the curator can revise the YAML in response to specific critic objections.

## 6. Conclusion

We have described and evaluated a reject-aware pipeline for cross-domain universality-class discovery, with a two-stage adversarial critic layer (single-model B1 + 3-model DeepSeek ensemble B3) inserted between LLM curation and empirical validation. On a 21-candidate panel, B1 rejected 14.3% of classes and the B3 ensemble rejected 33.3%. The four classes B3 rejected that B1 had voted KEEP are textbook mechanism-vs-surface-similarity failures, including a *mechanism-vs-limit-theorem* confusion on the delay-differential-debt class that B1 missed and two rigorous DeepSeek reviewers independently flagged. Two of the seven B3 rejections were corroborated downstream by real-data empirical tests, tying the methodology back to ground truth.

The pipeline cost approximately \$1.60 and 53 minutes wall-clock for 21 classes — three orders of magnitude under human reviewer time at expert rates. The architecture, prompts, code, and verdict matrix are publicly available; the methodology is reproducible end-to-end at sub-dollar marginal cost per panel.

We argue that *systematic reject layers* should be a methodological standard in cross-domain analogy programs, on the same footing as positive discovery — reported alongside KEEP rates, with full prompt and verdict traceability. The reject-aware framing forces an explicit accounting of false-positive control, and the multi-model ensemble structure makes the accounting cheap enough to do routinely.

## Appendix A. B3 prompt template (verbatim)

The following is the user-prompt template used for all three B3 reviewers (system prompts differ per reviewer; see §3.3). Curly-brace variables are filled from the Layer-3 YAML and B1 critic output for each class.

```
Review this candidate universality class for whether it forms a valid cross-domain
universality class in the dynamical-systems sense (shared equation form + shared
scaling exponents + shared critical mechanism).

Class id: {class_id}
Display name: {display_name}
Hub phenomenon: {hub}
Shared equation / normal form:
{shared_eq}

Key invariants:
{invariants}

Positive examples (members claimed by Layer-3 auto-curator):
{positive_examples}

Negative examples / boundary cases:
{negative_examples}

Notes from yaml:
{notes}

B1 critic prior verdict: {b1_verdict} (confidence: {b1_conf})
B1 critic rationale (summarized):
{b1_rationale}

Output JSON only, in this exact schema:
{
  "class_id": "{class_id}",
  "model_id": "<your-reviewer-id>",
  "verdict": "KEEP" | "REJECT" | "UNCLEAR" | "SPLIT" | "MERGE",
  "confidence": <float 0.0-1.0>,
  "rationale": "<2-4 sentences on why; cite mechanism + exponent + equation form>"
}

Decision guide:
- KEEP: shared mechanism + shared exponents (or shared dynamic equation form) supported by ≥3 positive members.
- REJECT: limit-theorem only / surface similarity / members from incompatible mechanisms.
- SPLIT: real class but heterogeneous; should be split into 2+ tighter classes.
- MERGE: this class is a near-duplicate of another standard class and should be merged.
- UNCLEAR: cannot determine from this brief; needs more empirical data.

Output ONLY the JSON object, no preamble or explanation outside the JSON.
```

The three reviewer system prompts are reproduced verbatim in §3.3 above.

## Appendix B. 21-class verdict matrix (machine-readable schema)

Each line of `v4/results/B3_taxonomy_v2.jsonl` is one JSON record with the following keys (illustrated for the `delay_differential_debt` case):

```json
{
  "class_id": "delay_differential_debt",
  "b1_verdict": "KEEP",
  "b1_confidence": "medium",
  "b3_verdicts": ["REJECT", "REJECT", "KEEP"],
  "b3_confidences": [0.8, 0.65, 0.8],
  "b3_consensus": "REJECT",
  "b3_avg_confidence": 0.75,
  "final_verdict": "CONTESTED(B1=KEEP,B3=REJECT)"
}
```

Order of `b3_verdicts` is fixed: `[deepseek-pro-rigorous, deepseek-flash-rigorous, deepseek-pro-creative]`. Confidence is on the canonical 0.0-1.0 scale. The `final_verdict` field uses these tags: `KEEP_strong` (B1 and B3 both KEEP), `REJECT_strong` (both REJECT), `SPLIT_strong` (both SPLIT), `MERGE_strong` (both MERGE), `CONTESTED(B1=X,B3=Y)` (categorical disagreement), `MIXED(B1=X,B3=Y)` (related but distinct, e.g., B1 KEEP / B3 MERGE), and `SPLIT+B3=REJECT` (B1 SPLIT / B3 REJECT — both reject monolithic class but differ on next step).

End of document.
