Why bank runs, earthquakes, and neural avalanches are the same thing

Three completely different systems collapse in eerily similar ways. Once you see the shape, you can’t un-see it — and you can start predicting where it’ll show up next.

≈ 1,650 words · 2026-05-13 · plain English · no equations

1. A puzzle that took a hundred years to spot

In 1906, San Francisco shook. In 1929, Wall Street panicked. In 1955, a neuroscientist named Donald Hebb noticed that the brains of awake cats crackle with tiny bursts of activity, separated by long quiet stretches. Three completely different domains, three completely different decades, three completely different scientific communities.

Nothing connects them. Except that when researchers eventually got enough data to plot the “how big” of each event — the magnitude of an earthquake, the size of a market crash, the duration of a brain burst — the curves all looked the same. Not similar. The same. A long flat tail of small events, vanishingly few giant ones, and a smooth fall-off that follows a precise mathematical shape called a power law.

The technical name for this shape is dry. The implication is not. It means the earth doesn’t care whether it’s shaking a fault line, a bank’s balance sheet, or a neuron. Below the surface of all three is the same kind of process, the same kind of dynamics, the same tendency to sit quietly and then release everything at once.

2. The pattern: living on the edge of a cliff

Imagine a pile of sand. Drop one grain at a time. For a while, nothing happens — the pile gets a little taller. Then suddenly one extra grain triggers an avalanche of a few hundred. The pile relaxes, and you start dropping again. Most of the time you get nothing. Occasionally you get a small slide. Once in a while, a wholesale collapse.

That sand pile isn’t doing anything fancy. It’s just stuck near a threshold — each grain pushes a local slope toward a tipping point, and when one local slope finally tips, it shoves its neighbors over too. Sometimes the neighbors absorb the shove and nothing else happens. Sometimes they pass it along, and so do their neighbors, and you get a cascade.

That’s the whole secret. A system made of many parts, each slowly building up local pressure, each connected to its neighbors — will sit calmly for long stretches, occasionally throwing off small shocks, and rarely (but inevitably) collapse all at once. Earthquakes work this way: the crust accumulates stress, locked faults pop and transfer load. Banks work this way: leverage builds up between counterparties until one failure forces another. Brains work this way: neurons charge until a few fire, which makes their neighbors fire.

The earth doesn’t care whether it’s shaking a fault line, a bank’s balance sheet, or a neuron. The same process produces the same statistics.

Physicists named this family of systems self-organized criticality. The phrase is unwieldy; the idea is simple: many systems, left alone, drift naturally to the edge of a phase transition and live there. They don’t need to be tuned. They find the edge by themselves. And once they’re there, the rules of how they fail are the same regardless of what they’re made of.

3. From “same statistics” to “same machinery”

A skeptic might say: So what? Lots of things look similar in cherry-picked summary statistics. That doesn’t mean they share a mechanism. Fair. So the way the physics community has dealt with this, slowly, since the 1970s, is to insist on more than one shared number. They look for a whole family of predictions that all have to line up.

For our sand-pile family, the predictions are:

(a) The size of avalanches follows a power law with an exponent in a narrow band.
(b) The time between avalanches follows another predictable shape (the aftershock decay rate that seismologists call Omori’s law).
(c) If you re-scale the system — squint at the data from far away — everything collapses onto a single universal curve.

If a new system passes all of these checks, not just the first one, that’s called sharing a universality class. The term sounds physics-y. The practical meaning: same fingerprint, same machinery. You can take tools developed in one field — say, seismology’s extensive theory of fault dynamics — and apply them to a different field where the data looks identical.

4. The pipeline: how we find these matches automatically

Researchers have been spotting these connections one at a time, slowly, since the 1980s. Each match takes a graduate student, a year, a paper. We wanted to know: can a computer do this systematically? Could you take a database of thousands of phenomena from dozens of disciplines and have the software propose — and then test — matches you didn’t know existed?

The pipeline we built does four things, end to end:

4,443 phenomena from 87 disciplines │ ▼ [1] Embed each phenomenon as a structural fingerprint. │ ▼ [2] Cluster fingerprints to surface candidate families. │ ▼ [3] Have multiple AI critics independently vote KEEP or REJECT on each candidate. Majority rules. │ ▼ [4] For survivors, run real-world data through the same code, no parameter tuning. Check if it fits the predictions. │ ▼ 13 verified systems · 5 confirmed universality classes

Layer 1 finds structural fingerprints. Layer 2 groups them. Layer 3 sanity-checks the groups with several independent reviewers. Layer 5 tests each survivor on real data with no tuning — one piece of code, many domains. The whole thing is open source.

The key discipline is layer 3: let the critics try to break things. Most failed matches in this field are not bold-faced errors — they’re plausible-looking groupings where someone confused a mathematical framework with an actual mechanism. (Confusing a hammer with a building, basically.) Running three independent reviewers and demanding majority agreement filters those out cheaply.

5. What we’ve found so far

Running the pipeline against open data sources, we’ve so far verified 13 independent systems falling into five universality classes. The verified list includes earthquakes (USGS catalogs), stock-market jumps (35 years of S&P 500), DeFi cascades (three protocols), neural avalanches (mouse cortex), bank failures (FDIC), wildfires (NIFC), solar flares (GOES), traffic phase transitions (NGSIM US-101), and lake regime shifts (Fox River). Same 339 lines of Python. No parameter tuning between domains.

We also ran four synthetic null datasets — data engineered to not be self-organized critical — through the same code. The pipeline correctly rejected all four. That’s the check that turns “interesting” into “trustworthy”: a tool that confirms everything you point it at is useless.

6. Why this matters outside academic curiosity

Three concrete reasons.

For investors: if a portfolio company has the statistical signature of a self-organized critical system, the reasonable expectation is rare-but-violent loss events — not a smooth bell curve. Risk models that assume bell curves consistently under-price tail risk in these systems. You can also tell, roughly, how close to the edge a particular system is sitting. We do this automatically for 100 public companies on the Phase Detector site.

For public policy: grid operators, regulators, public health officials all design interventions on assumptions about how systems behave between shocks. If the underlying process is critical cascading rather than independent random failures, the cheapest intervention isn’t “reduce average load” — it’s “reduce the tightness of coupling between parts.” That insight comes for free once you recognize the family.

For science itself: the entire history of physics has been finding two phenomena that look unrelated and showing that they share an underlying law. Light and electricity. Magnets and crystals. Heat and information. Every one of those mergers compressed a giant table of facts into a single equation. There’s no reason this should stop at physics. Biology, economics, ecology, social dynamics — the data is now big enough, and the tools good enough, to look systematically.
