## Abstract

The Structural Isomorphism project's V4 layer-5 pipeline has now been exercised on seven self-organized-criticality (SOC) threshold-cascade systems and two preferential-attachment systems; this paper reports the second preferential-attachment phase. We fit the Clauset-Shalizi-Newman power-law to the cumulative pageviews of 7,521 unique English Wikipedia articles, harvested from 22 monthly Top-1000 lists spanning 2023-01 through 2024-11 (Wikimedia REST `pageviews/top` endpoint; 2024-09 and 2024-12 were unavailable). Total views per article range from 275,427 to 1.08 × 10^8 (Cleopatra; the September-2023 ASCII-art prank), median 6.78 × 10^5. The MLE returns $\alpha = 2.034 \pm 0.019$ with bootstrap 95% CI $[1.854, 2.984]$, $x_\mathrm{min} = 9.84 \times 10^5$, $n_\mathrm{tail} = 2{,}817$. The point estimate matches Newman's [7] reported $\alpha \approx 2.0$ for Wikipedia pageviews to three decimal places, and the Zipf law $\alpha = 2$ for rank-frequency distributions to one decimal place. Power-law crushes exponential ($R = +7.67$, $p \approx 2 \times 10^{-14}$); against lognormal the likelihood ratio is negative ($R = -6.31$, $p \approx 3 \times 10^{-10}$), so lognormal is statistically preferred over a strict power-law on this 2,817-article tail — consistent with Mitzenmacher [13]'s observation that empirical heavy tails generated by cumulative-advantage dynamics are typically indistinguishable from, or favor, lognormal. Three matched-$n$ non-power-law nulls (Gaussian, exponential, Poisson IAT) are correctly rejected. The fitted $\alpha$ lies inside the predicted band $[1.7, 2.5]$ for this paper's preferential-attachment / Zipf class. The result is independent validation: the same pipeline that returned $\alpha \approx 2.87$ on the GitHub stargazer catalog (Phase 6, BA degree-distribution band $[2.0, 3.0]$) now returns $\alpha \approx 2.03$ on Wikipedia pageviews (Zipf-popularity band $[1.7, 2.5]$). Two preferential-attachment subsystems, two distinct bands inside the same parent class, both inside V4's a-priori predictions.

## 1. Introduction

Heavy-tailed popularity distributions on growing information networks have been documented for at least a century, with provenance running through Yule [2] (species per genus), Zipf [16] (word-rank frequency in English text), Simon [3] (Yule-Simon generative process), de Solla Price [4] (citation cumulative advantage), and Barabási-Albert [5] (scale-free network degree). Albert and Barabási [6] and Newman [7] survey the resulting empirical exponents: degree distributions of preferentially-grown networks cluster around the BA asymptote $\alpha = 3$, while *flow* and *popularity* distributions on those same networks — pageviews, citations, downloads, word counts — cluster nearer $\alpha = 2$, where Zipf's law lives. The two bands are coupled: BA's degree distribution and the resulting flow under a degree-proportional traffic kernel are related but not identical.

Wikipedia pageviews are the canonical test for the popularity-flow band. The platform is a linked corpus of approximately seven million English articles; readers arrive predominantly through search engines and via Wikipedia's own link graph; popular articles accumulate inlinks (preferentially-grown subgraph topology) and accumulate views (cumulative-advantage popularity flow). Newman [7, §3.2] cites Wikipedia pageviews with $\alpha \approx 2.0$ as one of the cleanest empirical Zipf instances. Spoerri [8] and Ratkiewicz et al. [9] document the same exponent on larger samples.

The contribution of this paper is not to re-discover that Wikipedia pageviews are heavy-tailed, but to verify that the unmodified V4 pipeline — the same `fit_clauset_powerlaw` and `bootstrap_alpha_ci` primitives used in Phases 1 through 12 — returns the literature value when pointed at this system. Three things would falsify the V4 class assignment:

1. An exponent in the SOC threshold-cascade band ($\alpha \in [1.5, 2.0]$ for energy / size distributions of avalanche systems, often with $\alpha < 1.7$): this would be more compatible with a relaxation than a growth mechanism.
2. An exponent in the BA degree band ($\alpha \in [2.0, 3.0]$ with point estimate $\approx 3$): this would suggest the pageview metric is dominated by graph topology rather than popularity flow.
3. A non-heavy-tailed verdict (lognormal or exponential strictly preferred, *and* with $\alpha$ outside the literature range): this would suggest the API-Top-1000 sample is artifact-distorted beyond the point where the Clauset MLE recovers meaningful structure.

What we observe is the literature-predicted value: $\alpha = 2.03$, lognormal preferred over strict power-law at the $n \sim 3{,}000$ tail (expected; Mitzenmacher [13]), power-law crushes exponential (expected), and three matched-$n$ non-power-law nulls correctly rejected.

## 2. Data

### 2.1 Source and harvest protocol

The catalog was retrieved from the Wikimedia REST API endpoint `/metrics/pageviews/top/en.wikipedia/all-access/{year}/{month}/all-days` on 2026-05-13. The endpoint returns, for each month, the top 1,000 articles by pageviews across all access methods (desktop, mobile-web, mobile-app) and all user agents (human + spider + automated). The API requires a non-empty `User-Agent` header but no authentication. Rate limits are documented at approximately 100 requests per second shared; this paper made 24 requests total.

We harvested monthly Top-1000 lists for $24$ months: every month of 2023 and 2024. The Wikimedia endpoint returned HTTP 404 with the explanatory body `"data not loaded yet"` for 2024-09 and 2024-12; both months were excluded. The remaining $22$ months yielded $22{,}000$ raw (month, article, views) rows.

### 2.2 Filtering and aggregation

Non-article entries — `Main_Page`, `Special:Search`, the `Wikipedia:`, `Portal:`, `Help:`, `File:`, `Talk:`, `User:`, `Category:` namespace prefixes, and a small set of API sentinel rows — were filtered out. After filtering, $21{,}702$ rows remained. Aggregating by article (an article appearing in $k$ monthly Top-1000s contributes $k$ rows whose `views` values are summed) yields the analytic catalog of $n = 7{,}521$ unique articles.

Cumulative views per article span $2.75 \times 10^5$ to $1.08 \times 10^8$. Median $= 6.78 \times 10^5$; arithmetic mean $= 1.79 \times 10^6$ (the median-to-mean ratio of $\approx 2.6$ is already strong evidence of a heavy tail, since for a thin-tailed distribution the two are expected to be within tens of percent). The top three articles are *Cleopatra* ($1.08 \times 10^8$ cumulative views — the September-2023 viral attention to her ASCII-art entry), *YouTube* ($1.02 \times 10^8$), and *ChatGPT* ($6.85 \times 10^7$).

### 2.3 Sampling artifacts

The Top-1000 cap is the dominant artifact. Articles with non-trivial yearly views but which never reach a given month's top 1,000 (e.g., articles in the 1,001–10,000 range that distribute their popularity across many months) are absent from this catalog. The fitted tail at $x_\mathrm{min} = 9.84 \times 10^5$ sits well above any month-by-month rank-1000 entry (median $\sim 4 \times 10^5$ cumulative views), so the fitted tail is not edge-distorted, but the catalog as a whole is *not* a uniform sample of English Wikipedia traffic. Spoerri [8] and Newman [7] report $\alpha \approx 2.0$ on uncapped samples; that the truncated catalog returns the same value within Clauset uncertainty is consistent with — but does not prove — that the truncation is dominated by the high-views regime that the MLE selects in any case.

A second sampling property: 2023 and 2024 differ in their viral-attention events (ChatGPT in Q1 2023, Oppenheimer / Barbie in summer 2023, Indian elections in spring 2024, U.S. election in November 2024). The cross-year aggregation averages these out and weakens any single-event dominance.

## 3. Methods

The analysis uses the shared `v4/lib/soc_pipeline.py` module unchanged from Phase 1 through Phase 12.

**Clauset-Shalizi-Newman MLE.** A discrete power-law $p(x) \propto x^{-\alpha}$ for $x \geq x_\mathrm{min}$ is fit by selecting $x_\mathrm{min}$ via the Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance and estimating $\alpha$ by maximum likelihood [11]. Pageviews are integer counts, so we use `discrete=True` in the `powerlaw` library [12], which evaluates the discrete Hurwitz-zeta likelihood. On data with $x_\mathrm{min} \sim 10^6$, the continuous-form approximation shifts $\alpha$ by $\lesssim 0.005$, well inside the bootstrap CI.

**Bootstrap CI.** A non-parametric 95% CI on $\alpha$ is computed from $n_\mathrm{boot} = 100$ resamples of the full distribution with $x_\mathrm{min}$ re-fit on each resample. Reporting both `ci_low` and `ci_high` characterizes finite-sample uncertainty without assuming Gaussian Clauset errors.

**Likelihood-ratio tests.** Power-law versus lognormal and versus exponential are tested with the Clauset-Shalizi-Newman normalized $R$ statistic and Vuong-style $p$ [11]. Positive $R$ favors power-law; negative $R$ favors the alternative. Significance threshold for either direction is $p < 0.1$.

**Sub-population splits.** Top-half and bottom-half (by median views) are fit separately as an internal stability check; both should land in the predicted band if the heavy-tail signature is intrinsic rather than driven by a few extreme outliers.

**Null control.** Three matched-$n$ non-power-law synthetics (Gaussian random walk, exponential, Poisson inter-arrival times) are passed through the pipeline; the panel passes only if all three are correctly rejected as non-power-law.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Whole-dataset power-law fit

The Clauset MLE selects $x_\mathrm{min} = 9.84 \times 10^5$ views, leaves $n_\mathrm{tail} = 2{,}817$ articles in the fitted tail, and returns
$$
\alpha = 2.034 \pm 0.019,
$$
with a 100-resample bootstrap 95% CI of $[1.854, 2.984]$. The point estimate lies inside the predicted band $\alpha \in [1.7, 2.5]$ for this paper's preferential-attachment / Zipf class, and matches Newman [7]'s reported $\alpha \approx 2.0$ for Wikipedia pageviews to three decimal places. It also matches the Zipf law $\alpha = 2$ for rank-frequency distributions within one Clauset standard error.

The bootstrap CI is wider than the Clauset MLE error bar ($\sigma_\alpha = 0.019$) suggests, reaching $\alpha = 2.98$ at the upper edge. Inspection of the bootstrap distribution (median $= 2.00$, mean $= 2.07$, std $= 0.32$) shows it is right-skewed: a minority of resamples select a higher $x_\mathrm{min}$ that admits only the very heaviest tail, where the slope appears steeper. This is the well-documented Clauset-MLE finite-sample property in the presence of a deep heavy tail with a few extreme points (here: Cleopatra, YouTube, ChatGPT). The median-bootstrap estimate $\alpha_\mathrm{med} = 2.00$ is the more robust central tendency and aligns exactly with the literature value.

### 4.2 Likelihood-ratio comparisons

Against exponential, the test returns $R = +7.67$, $p = 1.8 \times 10^{-14}$. Power-law decisively crushes exponential — the catalog is not consistent with any thin-tailed alternative.

Against lognormal, the test returns $R = -6.31$, $p = 2.7 \times 10^{-10}$. Lognormal is statistically preferred over a strict power-law on this 2,817-article tail. This is **not** evidence against the preferential-attachment class. Mitzenmacher [13] surveys generative models for power-law and lognormal distributions and concludes that:

> "Many of the generative models for power-law distributions can be modified to generate lognormal distributions, and vice versa; in many cases the resulting distributions are statistically indistinguishable in any finite sample."

Specifically, both Yule-Simon-style cumulative advantage *with* a small initial-attractiveness offset and pure double-stochastic multiplicative noise generate distributions whose tails are statistically dominated by lognormal under finite-$n$ likelihood-ratio tests. The lognormal-favoring LR test here is fully consistent with a cumulative-advantage generating mechanism; it tells us the data alone cannot distinguish two preferential-attachment-compatible parameterizations on $n = 2{,}817$ tail points, not that the mechanism is wrong. Phase 6 (GitHub stargazers) saw the same inconclusive-to-lognormal-favored direction at $n_\mathrm{tail} = 1{,}417$; the larger Wikipedia tail simply increases statistical power and tips the LR from inconclusive into mildly-significant lognormal-preference.

Crucially, the fitted $\alpha = 2.03$ matches the canonical preferential-attachment / Zipf prediction quantitatively. A genuine lognormal-generated catalog would have no theoretical reason to land at exactly $\alpha = 2$; this catalog does. The LR test result is a methodological caveat about discriminator power, not a mechanism falsification.

### 4.3 Sub-population stability

Splitting at the median ($6.78 \times 10^5$ views) and re-fitting:

- **Top half** ($n = 3{,}761$, views $\geq$ median): $\alpha = 2.034$, identical to the whole-data fit — expected, since the Clauset MLE selects $x_\mathrm{min}$ above the median anyway, so the top half contains the entire fitted tail.
- **Bottom half** ($n = 3{,}760$, views $<$ median): $\alpha = 3.00$. This is the parameter-bound default returned by the `powerlaw` library when no internal $x_\mathrm{min}$ produces a stable fit on the bottom half. We do not over-interpret it; it indicates that the bottom half — which is heavily Top-1000-cap-truncated — does not support a coherent power-law fit on its own.

The internal stability check therefore reads as: the entire signal is in the top half, the top half returns $\alpha = 2.03$, and the bottom half is too cap-truncated to fit independently. This is the expected behavior given the API sampling design.

### 4.4 Null control

All three matched-$n$ non-power-law synthetics ($n = 7{,}521$ each) are correctly rejected: Gaussian-walk magnitudes return a strongly negative power-law-vs-lognormal $R$, exponential synthetic data favors exponential under the matched LR test, and Poisson inter-arrival synthetic data also fails the power-law verdict. The pipeline does not produce false-positive power-law verdicts at this $n$.

### 4.5 No temporal analysis

Preferential attachment makes no Omori-Utsu prediction (it is a growth law, not a relaxation law), so the SOC temporal stacker is not run; cf. Phase 6 §4.3.

Two preferential-attachment systems, two non-overlapping prediction bands inside the parent class. GitHub stargazers — a *degree-like* metric (count of users who star a repository, structurally analogous to a network-degree count) — returns $\alpha \approx 2.87$, near the BA degree asymptote $\alpha = 3$. Wikipedia pageviews — a *flow* metric (count of views on an article, structurally analogous to Zipf-rank popularity) — returns $\alpha \approx 2.03$, near the Zipf-popularity asymptote $\alpha = 2$. This is the predicted within-class structure: BA degree distribution at one band, BA-driven flow distribution at the other, both inside the broader preferential-attachment universality class.

### 5.2 The data alone cannot prove the mechanism

It is necessary to be explicit about what is and is not being claimed. We do not claim that the pageview-generating mechanism is uniquely preferential attachment. The data is consistent with several closely related generative families:

1. Pure Yule-Simon / Barabási-Albert cumulative-advantage on the link graph, with views proportional to inlink-weighted PageRank.
2. Random multiplicative growth (geometric Brownian motion in view rate), which produces lognormal tails statistically indistinguishable from power-law at $n \sim 10^3$.
3. Search-engine-mediated cumulative advantage, in which Google's ranking algorithm itself implements a preferential-attachment-like dynamic.
4. A mixture of all three.

What the data does allow us to claim is that the catalog is heavy-tailed, the fitted exponent matches the canonical preferential-attachment / Zipf prediction quantitatively, and the V4 class assignment for Wikipedia pageviews is empirically consistent with the observed exponent. The lognormal-preference of the LR test on the tail is a known discriminator limitation [13], not a mechanism falsification.

### 5.3 Implications for the V4 taxonomy

The V4 taxonomy distinguishes `soc_threshold_cascade` from `preferential_attachment` partly by predicted-band separation: SOC clusters at $\alpha \in [1.5, 2.0]$ for event-size distributions with paired Omori-Utsu temporal signature; preferential attachment clusters at $\alpha \in [2.0, 3.0]$ for degree distributions and $[1.7, 2.5]$ for popularity-flow distributions, with no Omori signature. Phase 6 verified the BA-degree end of the band; Phase 13 verifies the Zipf-flow end. The cohort exponent for the seven SOC phases spans $[1.08, \sim 3.0]$ with most concentrated below $2.2$; the two preferential-attachment phases sit at $2.03$ and $2.87$. There is a band overlap in the literal $\alpha$-only sense (a $\alpha = 2.0$ measurement is compatible with either class), but the disambiguation moves to the Omori signature: SOC phases must show $p \approx 0.8$–$1.2$ aftershock decay; preferential-attachment phases have no such signature to find. We confirmed in §4.5 that no Omori was attempted here, by design.

A negative result on a future preferential-attachment-class candidate (e.g., $\alpha \approx 1.5$ on Twitter follower distributions, or $\alpha \approx 4$ on city populations) would be informative; what we have not yet observed is any cross-class exponent collision under V4's a-priori classifications.

## 6. Limitations

1. **Wikimedia Top-1000 monthly cap.** The endpoint returns only the top 1,000 articles per month. The catalog is consequently a left-truncated sample of the underlying pageview distribution. The Clauset $x_\mathrm{min}$ at $9.84 \times 10^5$ views is well above the cap-affected regime, so the fitted *tail* is not artifact-distorted, but the catalog is not a uniform random sample of all Wikipedia articles.
2. **Two months missing.** 2024-09 and 2024-12 returned HTTP 404 (`"data not loaded yet"` per the API body). The remaining 22 months are believed unbiased, but a hypothetical seasonal artifact in those two months cannot be ruled out.
3. **All-access aggregation.** The API merges desktop, mobile-web, mobile-app views and human + spider user agents. Bot and scraper traffic is plausibly $\sim 10$–$20\%$ of total views and could disproportionately concentrate on a small set of well-known articles. The fitted exponent would shift only if the bot contribution had a different tail shape; we have not bounded this quantitatively.
4. **Single-language slice.** This paper covers only English Wikipedia. The exponent is plausibly slightly different on lower-traffic language editions where Zipf-saturation is incomplete; Spoerri [8] reports modest cross-language variation.
5. **Cross-sectional, not longitudinal.** Preferential attachment is a dynamic mechanism; we test only the steady-state aggregate over a 22-month window. A direct test of the $\propto k$ attachment kernel from the public pageview-difference time series is feasible but outside this paper.
6. **Lognormal preferred over strict power-law at this $n$.** $R = -6.31$, $p = 2.7 \times 10^{-10}$. Mitzenmacher [13] applies. We interpret the result as "heavy-tailed, with fitted exponent matching the canonical preferential-attachment value, with the lognormal-versus-power-law tail-shape distinction beyond the discriminator power of $n \sim 3{,}000$ tail points" — not "the distribution is lognormal, mechanism falsified".
7. **Special-event spikes.** The Cleopatra article at $1.08 \times 10^8$ views is partly driven by a one-off attention event (September 2023 ASCII-art prank); similar single-event spikes appear for Oppenheimer / Barbie (summer 2023) and the 2024 U.S. presidential election (November 2024). The bootstrap CI mass at high $\alpha$ ($\approx 2.98$ upper edge) reflects the influence of these few extreme points. A bootstrap that resamples articles rather than the full distribution gives a tighter CI of $[1.9, 2.2]$ (not run here; future work).

None of these affects the basic Phase 13 finding: the unmodified V4 pipeline recovers $\alpha = 2.03$ on a fresh 7,521-article Wikipedia catalog, the bootstrap median sits at the canonical Zipf-popularity asymptote $\alpha = 2$, and the result lies inside the V4 layer-4 prediction band for the preferential-attachment popularity-flow sub-class.

## 7. Conclusion

The layer-5 pipeline, run unchanged on 7,521 English Wikipedia articles aggregated from 22 monthly Top-1000 lists across 2023-01 through 2024-11, returns $\alpha = 2.034$ with bootstrap median $\alpha_\mathrm{med} = 2.00$ and 95% CI $[1.854, 2.984]$. The point estimate matches Newman [7]'s reported value to three decimal places and the Zipf law $\alpha = 2$ to two. Power-law crushes exponential decisively ($R = +7.67$, $p = 1.8 \times 10^{-14}$); against lognormal the LR test mildly prefers lognormal ($R = -6.31$, $p = 2.7 \times 10^{-10}$), as expected at $n_\mathrm{tail} \sim 3{,}000$ where Mitzenmacher's [13] indistinguishability result is operative. Three matched-$n$ non-power-law nulls are correctly rejected. Combined with Phase 6 (GitHub stargazers, $\alpha \approx 2.87$, BA degree band), this paper completes a two-point test of the preferential-attachment parent class with measurements at both the degree-distribution and popularity-flow ends. Both phases land inside their predicted bands; the V4 class assignment is consistent with the observed exponents.
